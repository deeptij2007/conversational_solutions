### Tips for Saving on Car Insurance Premiums

Are you a driver who thinks saving on your car insurance premium is impossible? Think again! With these ten tips, you'll discover practical ways to cut costs and ensure you're only paying for the coverage you truly need.

### 1. The Model of Your Car Can Help You Save

The type of vehicle you drive plays a significant role in determining your insurance premium. Sports cars and rare models are often more expensive to insure due to their higher risk of theft. Cars may be more expensive than SUVs. Some SUVs are considered high theft risk while others are not. 

When you are considering purchasing a vehicle and you are considering several different models and/or manufacturers, call your insurer first to see which model has the lowest insurance premium. You may be able to save yourself several hundred dollars a year in insurance costs.

### 2. Think Anti-Theft Discount

Installing approved anti-theft devices , anti-starter systems, or tracking technology in your vehicle can lead to significant savings on your insurance premium. Insurers may offer an anti-theft discount , rewarding your proactive measures to protect your car. Contact your insurer to learn more about eligible anti-theft device car installations and how you can benefit from this discount.

### 3. Shop Around and Weigh Your Options

Don't settle for the first insurance quote you receive. Comparing different insurers is crucial to finding the best premium. Prices can fluctuate based on available programs, so it's wise to periodically reassess your coverage options. Sometimes, simply contacting your current insurer and asking how to lower car insurance can yield positive results.

### 4. Get a Tailor-Made Policy

Customize your policy to reflect your specific needs and driving habits. Eliminate unnecessary coverage to reduce costs—remember, the more protections included, the higher the premium. Additionally, consider your annual mileage; driving less can often lead to discount car insurance rates.

### 5. Drive Safely

Many insurers reward safe driving practices by offering car insurance discounts to drivers who have maintained a claim-free record for several years. Programs such as automerit analyze driving data, providing additional opportunities to save money while promoting safer roads. Driving cautiously not only prevents accidents but also helps reduce insurance expenses.

### 6. Look for Exclusive Member Discounts

Belairdirect offers exclusive discounts for various professional groups, including healthcare providers, educators, and alumni. As a member of an eligible group, you might qualify for substantial savings on your car insurance.

### 7. Get a Multi-Vehicle Discount

If you insure more than one vehicle with the same provider, you might be eligible for a multi-vehicle discount . This can lead to significant reductions in your overall insurance costs, offering convenience and savings for households with multiple cars.

### 8. Students Can Save

Students have unique insurance needs, and there are discounts for students tailored specifically for them. If you're a student or have one in your household, inquire about potential savings on your car insurance. Belairdirect provides options that cater to student drivers, including reduced premiums for maintaining good grades.

### 9. Bundle Your Home and Auto Insurance

Many insurers offer bundling discounts when you combine multiple types of policies—such as home and auto—under one provider. This approach not only simplifies your insurance management but also results in cost savings. Learn more about bundling your insurance .

### 10. Drive a Hybrid or Electric Vehicle

Driving a hybrid or electric vehicle may qualify you for special discounts in select areas. These eco-friendly options are often favored by insurers due to their lower environmental impact and can lead to reduced premiums. 

By implementing these tips, you can maximize savings on car insurance while ensuring comprehensive coverage. Explore more strategies at belairdirect and take control of your car insurance costs today!

### How to store your RV for winter

If you own an RV, you know that winter storage isn’t just a seasonal chore, it’s how you protect your investment and make sure your spring travels get off to a smooth start. Canada’s cold weather can be tough on things like the plumbing, tires, and batteries, so taking the time now can help prevent costly repairs when spring rolls around.

Your recreational vehicle could be a motorized or a towable trailer. At belairdirect, we insure the towable kind like travel trailers, fifth-wheels, and tent-trailers, so while this guide focuses mainly on them, our practical storage tips can certainly apply to motorhomes too.

### Preparing your RV water system for winter

Few things damage an RV faster than frozen water lines. Even a small amount of water left behind can expand, crack pipes, or damage tanks once the temperatures drop. Here’s what to do:

- Start by draining the fresh, grey, and black water tanks completely, then rinse them to prevent buildup and odours.

- Once the tanks are empty, switch off the water heater, let it cool, and drain it as well.

- If your RV has a bypass valve for the water heater, turn it to the winter setting so you don’t waste antifreeze filling the tank.

- The next step is clearing the water lines. Opening the low-point drains beneath the RV helps remove most of the remaining water, and briefly running the water pump pushes out whatever’s left in the system.

- Remove any water filters for the season so antifreeze doesn’t run through them.

- Once everything is drained, it’s time for RV-approved, non-toxic antifreeze. Running the faucets one by one until you see the pink solution ensures the entire system is protected.

- Don’t forget the toilet, shower, and sink traps too!

### Interior and exterior winter preparations for your RV

A fresh, dry interior makes spring opening much easier. Get prepped with this simple checklist:

- Remove all food and perishables, including sealed pantry items to prevent pests.

- Give the inside a thorough clean, defrost the fridge, and wipe down surfaces so no moisture gets trapped. Many RV owners also use moisture absorbers or a small dehumidifier to help prevent mould over the winter.

- Keep cupboard doors and interior spaces open to promote airflow and keeps the whole RV smelling better.

Next, follow these steps for the outside:

- Give the exterior a good wash to help protect your RV’s finish from dirt and road residue that can cause deterioration. Adding a coat of wax creates an extra barrier against winter moisture.

- Inspect the seals around windows, vents, doors, and slide-outs. If you notice cracks or worn areas, repair them before storing the vehicle.

- Covering your RV is also important. A breathable RV-specific cover keeps out snow and debris without trapping in moisture, which is something tarps can do. If your RV is stored outdoors, this small step can make a big difference.

### Caring for your RV engine, tires, and battery

If you own a motorized RV, winter storage includes a few extra steps for your engine. Adding fuel stabilizer helps prevent the gas from breaking down over time, and topping up fluids like coolant and oil can help protect the engine from corrosion while it sits. Be sure to run the engine briefly after adding the stabilizer to circulate everything properly.

Whether your RV is motorized or towable, good tire care is essential. Inflate them to the manufacturer’s recommended pressure before storing the vehicle, especially if it’ll stay parked for several months. UV-resistant tire covers help protect the rubber from cracking from winter sun exposure. And if you’re able to, moving the RV slightly every month or so can help avoid flat spots.

Batteries also need attention. Disconnecting them prevents slow electrical drain, and storing them in a warmer indoor space prolongs their lifespan. Giving the battery a charge every so often over the winter keeps it ready for spring.

### What does RV insurance cover?

The other kind of protection for your RV is, of course, insurance. RV insurance varies depending on whether your RV is towable or motorized.

### Towable RV coverage

At belairdirect, we offer coverage for towable RVs with our RV Comfort Plan which includes protection against accidental physical damage, theft, vandalism, fire, and certain weather-related losses. Since towable RVs rely on the towing vehicle for movement, liability coverage comes from your auto insurance policy.

The RV Comfort Plan also includes up to $7,500 for personal property, coverage for permanently installed accessories, emergency roadside assistance, towing and repatriation, and additional living expenses if your RV can’t be used following a covered loss. This coverage is available to you in PEI, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, Quebec, and Newfoundland.

### Motorized RV coverage

Motorhomes must be insured with a standard auto insurance policy because they’re motor vehicles. At belairdirect, our motorized RV coverage isn’t available in Alberta, Ontario, or Atlantic Canada, and in Quebec, motorized RVs still require a dedicated auto policy, with optional coverage for contents, roadside assistance, equipment, and more.

### How to get RV storage insurance?

If you’re storing your RV for the winter, it’s important to know what coverage applies while the vehicle is parked. Some owners assume they can pause coverage during the off-season, but damage can still occur, especially from theft, fire, vandalism, or weather-related events.

Getting the right protection is simple. Start by contacting your insurer and asking about the options available for your type of RV. Provide plenty of details about where and how the RV will be stored, since your location can affect your coverage needs.

Costs vary widely based on your RV’s value, the type of coverage you choose, and the province you live in. Comparing quotes also helps you choose a plan that fits your budget and gives you the confidence that your RV is protected all year.

### A little care goes a long way

Preparing your RV for winter might take a bit of effort now, but it can save you time, money, and stress when spring arrives! And with the right insurance, you can store your RV confidently knowing it’s protected through the coldest months of the year.

Reach out for a quote or compare your options online. Insurance is important, and it shouldn’t be complicated, so we’re here to help you keep things simple.

### FAQs

### 1. Do I need insurance while my RV is in storage?

Yes. Risks like theft, fire, and weather damage exist year-round, even when your RV isn’t on the road.

### 2. Can I store my RV outside for the winter?

Yes, as long as you use a breathable cover, protect your tires, and store it on level ground. Some municipalities have specific rules, so check your local bylaws.

### 3. Does RV insurance cover snow or ice damage?

It may, depending on the type of coverage you have and whether the damage was sudden and accidental.

### Ontario vehicle registration: a complete guide

Getting a new car is a great feeling, and registering it is one of the steps that helps you get on the road as soon as possible. Once you know what the province needs from you, you’ll be able to get everything done quickly and easily. Whether you’re buying a new car from a dealership, picking up a used car from a private seller, or dealing with something a little more unique like a gifted or inherited vehicle, we’ll walk you through all the steps, and give you some helpful examples and tips to help make the process smoother from start to finish.

### What documents do I need?

Most registration scenarios in Ontario call for the same pieces of information: who you are, whether the vehicle is insured, and whether you can prove ownership. Depending on the type of vehicle and how you got it, you may also need a Safety Standards Certificate.

Your valid driver’s licence covers identification, while your insurance papers confirm your policy is active. Ownership documents vary, like a bill of sale for private purchases, a New Vehicle Information Statement (NVIS) for dealership purchases, or estate documents if you inherited the vehicle. Most used or out-of-province vehicles also require a Safety Standards Certificate to show they meet Ontario’s safety rules.

Before heading to ServiceOntario, gather everything and make sure names, VINs, and purchase details match across all documents. A quick review can save you a second trip.

### How do I register a vehicle from a dealership in Ontario?

Registering a new (or used) vehicle from a dealership is usually the simplest scenario. Dealerships handle almost every detail for you, including the registration paperwork, the new plates or plate transfer, the required fees, and the documentation that must be submitted to the province.

Your main job is to provide your proof of insurance, your ID, and a few signatures. By the time you leave the dealership, the registration is complete and your new vehicle is legally ready for the road!

If the dealership does not complete the registration, you will have to register the vehicle at a Service Ontario centre. This Application for Vehicle Registration form (SR-LV-006) can be completed before visiting a Service Ontario.

### How do I register a used vehicle bought privately in Ontario?

Buying a used car from a private seller comes with a few extra steps. To register a vehicle and get a permit and licence plate, you need:

- either a drivers licence or an acceptable proof of identity document with legal name, date of birth and signature

- the completed used vehicle information package (only when registering a passenger vehicle, light commercial vehicle, self-propelled motor home or motorcycle)

- proof of purchase (for example, a receipt from the seller or a bill of sale from a used vehicle information package)

- proof of insurance from an insurance provider licensed in Ontario

- the original vehicle permit from the seller with completed transfer portion

- the plate portion of the permit, if you already have a licence plate you would like to attach to the vehicle

To ensure your vehicle is fit for the road, you will need:

- Safety Standards Certificate (not required for trailers, mopeds, off-road vehicles or snowmobiles)

- if you are registering a pick-up truck and trailer with a registered gross weight over 4,500 kilograms and will be used for personal use, you may need to provide the  Annual/Semi-Annual Inspection Declaration Form

Registration must be completed within six days of the sale at a Service Ontario centre, so it’s best to book your insurance and inspection quickly.

### Other registration scenarios

Not every registration fits neatly into “new” or “used.” Some situations require additional documents or steps, but the same basic principles always apply: prove ownership, arrange insurance, and meet Ontario’s safety standards.

### How to register a gifted vehicle in Ontario

Gifting a vehicle to eligible family members can mean you’re exempt from paying tax, which makes this scenario unique. To qualify, the giver and receiver must fall within specific relationships, such as parent-child, spouse-spouse, or siblings.

The following are eligible family members without them required to the retail sales tax (RST):

- spouse (including a common law spouse)

- parent or step-parent

- grandparent or step-grandparent

- son or step-son

- daughter or step-daughter

- grandson or step-grandson

- granddaughter or step-granddaughter

- son-in-law

- daughter-in-law

- father-in-law

- mother-in-law

- sibling

- half siblings (siblings with a common parent)

- adopted siblings (siblings with a common parent through adoption)

What documents are required. You will need to bring the following documents to a ServiceOntario centre:

- a completed Sworn Statement for a Family Gift of a Used Motor Vehicle in the Province of Ontario form

- proof of Ontario vehicle insurance

- your Ontario driver’s licence

- Safety Standards Certificate (this is not required for spouse-to-spouse transfers, but you must complete a spousal declaration)

- Vehicle Ownership Permit with the completed portion on the back

- a completed plate transfer declaration if you are also transferring the licence plate (only permitted between spouses, parents and children)

Only one exempt transfer of the same vehicle, between family members, is allowed within a 12-month period.

Additionally, if the vehicle acquired from a spouse or former spouse was part of a settlement under the Family Law Act, it may be exempt from the retail sales tax (RST).

A supporting document that states that the vehicle was part of the settlement is required. You will need to bring one of the following documents to a Service Ontario centre at the time of registration:

- a completed Sworn Statement for the Transfer of a Used Motor Vehicle in the Province of Ontario

- separation agreement

- divorce agreement

- other similar court document

### How to register an out-of-province vehicle in Ontario

If you’re bringing a vehicle into Ontario, whether you moved or purchased it elsewhere, there are a few extra steps. Service Ontario must also confirm the sale of new vehicles purchased from outside of Ontario with the out-of-province dealership prior to registration.

First, you’ll need Ontario ID and insurance. Most out-of-province vehicles must pass a safety inspection before they can be registered. You’ll also provide the previous jurisdiction’s ownership documents and possibly a valuation if the vehicle changed hands in a private sale.

Once everything is gathered, you can visit ServiceOntario to register, pay the necessary taxes, and receive your Ontario permit and plates.

### How to register a vehicle from an estate in Ontario

Registering a vehicle from an estate involves a combination of standard documents and estate-specific paperwork. You will typically need the existing vehicle permit, insurance, and, if required, a Safety Standards Certificate. Estate documents such as the will, a death certificate, or proof that you are the executor or beneficiary are also needed. Once these are provided, the vehicle can be transferred legally into your name.

### What are some tips to make registration smoother?

Small steps can make the process much easier. Printed copies of your documents, especially the bill of sale, can save time if digital files aren’t accepted. Double-check VINs and names across every document to avoid delays. And if you’re transferring plates, keep your old insurance active until the transfer is complete. Plates must remain linked to an insured vehicle until you officially reassign them.

### Deadlines to look out for

There are some key deadlines to note in Ontario:

- Private sales must be registered within six days of purchase.

- Moving? You have 14 days to update the address on your driver’s licence and vehicle permit.

And even without plate stickers for most passenger vehicles, registration still expires and needs to be renewed. Missing that renewal deadline means your vehicle is considered unregistered, which could result in fines and prevent you from legally driving until everything is updated.

### Get ready to drive your new ride

Registering a vehicle in Ontario comes down to a few essentials: proving you own the car, showing it’s insured, and making sure it’s safe for the road. Once you know which documents and steps apply to your situation, the process becomes much more manageable. Keep everything organized, watch your timelines, and you’ll be ready to hit the road in no time.

Reach out and we’ll be happy to give you a quote for the coverage you need for your new car.

### FAQs

### What is needed to register a car in Ontario?

You’ll typically need proof of identity (valid driver’s licence), proof of insurance, ownership documents, and for most used vehicles, a Safety Standards Certificate.

### How much does it cost to register my car in Ontario?

Costs depend on whether you’re getting new plates or transferring existing ones. Taxes are based on the purchase price or the vehicle’s wholesale value.

### How do I register an out-of-province vehicle in Ontario?

You’ll need Ontario insurance and ID, ownership documents from the previous province, a safety inspection, and a valuation before visiting ServiceOntario.

### Do I need a safety to register a vehicle in Ontario?

Most used vehicles require a Safety Standards Certificate. New vehicles bought from a dealership do not.

### How long do I have to register a vehicle in Ontario?

You have six days to register a vehicle purchased through a private sale.

### What happens if I miss the registration deadline?

You may face fines, and the vehicle cannot be legally driven until the registration is completed or renewed.

### Red-light camera tickets: what drivers need to know

Most drivers have experienced it at least once: you’re approaching an intersection, the light turns yellow faster than you expected, and suddenly there’s a flash. That split-second uncertainty often leads to a bigger question later: Was that a red-light camera? And am I getting a ticket?

Red-light cameras exist to help keep intersections safer. They’re designed to discourage drivers from entering an intersection after the light has turned red, a behaviour that’s linked to some of the most serious and dangerous collisions. And while getting a ticket in the mail is never fun, understanding how these cameras work, and what their tickets actually mean, can help you stay informed, prepared, and confident behind the wheel.

To give you some context, according to Transport Canada, approximately 27% of all traffic fatalities occur at intersections. Red-light cameras are one way cities try to reduce those numbers and encourage safer habits at busy cross streets.

Whether you’ve received a notice in the mail, spotted a camera and want to know how it works, or just want to avoid a costly surprise, let’s go over everything you need to know about red-light camera tickets in Canada.

### What are red-light camera tickets?

Red-light camera tickets are issued to the registered owner of a vehicle when a camera system captures the vehicle entering an intersection after the light has turned red. These systems are often installed at high-risk intersections where collisions tend to happen more frequently.

Red-light cameras use embedded sensors or radar to detect the movement of a vehicle once the signal has switched to red. When a car enters the intersection too late, the cameras take photographs (and sometimes video) of the rear of the vehicle, including a clear shot of the licence plate. These images are later reviewed by officials, and if the violation is confirmed, a ticket is mailed to the registered owner of the vehicle.

The goal isn’t to punish honest mistakes, it’s to discourage dangerous behaviour that puts everyone on the road at risk. The deterrent effect is the true purpose.

### Key differences between camera and police-issued tickets

Not all traffic tickets are treated the same. Red-light camera tickets operate under different rules than tickets handed out by police officers. Understanding this distinction is important, especially when it comes to insurance and your driving record.

Here’s how they compare:

 | Feature
 | Red-Light Camera Ticket
 | Police-Issued Ticket

 | Issued to
 | Vehicle owner
 | Driver

 | Demerit points
 | None
 | Yes, depending on offence

 | Driving record impact
 | Not recorded
 | Stays on record for 3 years

 | Insurance impact
 | Generally none
 | Can increase rates

 | Offence type
 | Administrative violation
 | Traffic offence

These differences matter because red-light camera tickets target the violation itself, not who was driving. That’s why no demerit points are added and why your driving record stays clean.

### Issued to the vehicle owner

One of the biggest distinctions with red-light camera tickets is who receives them. These tickets are issued to the vehicle owner, who is not necessarily the person driving at the time. That means that if you lent your car to a friend or family member then the ticket still appears under your name.

This setup is intentional. Since cameras can’t identify the driver, the law holds the owner responsible for ensuring the vehicle is operated safely and legally. It also keeps the enforcement process simple, there’s no investigation into who was behind the wheel.

### No demerit points: a notable distinction

Because red-light camera tickets are considered administrative violations, not driver violations, they do not result in demerit points. This matters because demerit points are tied to risky or illegal driving behaviours by an individual. Since the camera only confirms that the vehicle entered on red, not who was driving, provinces treat it as a non-driver offence.

No demerit points mean the violation does not appear on your driving record, and there are no escalating penalties tied to your licence. For many drivers, this is a major relief.

### Insurance impact: generally neutral

The good news is that red-light camera tickets usually do not affect your insurance rates. Because they are not added to your driving record, insurers have no visibility into these tickets.

To compare, police-issued red-light tickets can lead to higher insurance rates, higher risk classifications, and even potential surcharges on renewal. Insurance companies assess your driving risk based on violations tied to you, not your car. Since camera tickets don’t identify the driver, the industry treats them differently.

### Provincial differences in red-light camera fines

While the system works similarly across Canada, fines vary by province. Here are approximate costs for red light camera fines:

- Ontario: $325

- Alberta: $405

- British Columbia: $167

These numbers may change depending on the municipality and administrative fees. That’s why it’s always wise to double-check your local regulations or consult your ticket notice for the exact amount. The important takeaway is that the fine may sting, but the long-term consequences are limited.

### The consequences of ignoring red-light camera fines

Ignoring a red-light camera ticket won’t make it disappear. In fact, it usually leads to more hassle, and more cost. If you don’t pay, additional fees and late penalties can grow over time, and you likely won’t be able to renew your vehicle registration or licence until the fine is resolved.

The best approach is simple: if you believe the ticket is correct, pay it promptly. If you don’t, file a dispute within the allowed timeframe so the issue doesn’t follow you into your next renewal cycle.

### Disputing a red-light camera ticket

You can dispute a red-light camera ticket if you believe it was issued incorrectly. While not every case is successful, there are valid grounds to challenge a notice.

Common reasons for dispute include:

- The vehicle was stolen at the time of the violation

- Camera malfunction or unclear signage

- Incorrect details on the vehicle registration

- Obvious errors in the photo evidence

If you plan to dispute, start by reviewing the ticket carefully. Check the time, date, intersection, and plate information. Next, examine the photo evidence. Many municipalities offer online access to the images. Gather any supporting documentation that may apply like any police reports, insurance records, or repair invoices related to the incident. Lastly, submit your dispute on time, which is within 15–30 days in most provinces. If ever your case is complex, feel free to consider legal advice.

### Reducing the risk of red-light violations

While red-light cameras can feel frustrating, they’re intended to keep intersections safer, and the simplest way to avoid a ticket is to drive carefully and stay aware.

Here are a few practical habits that help:

- Stay alert at intersections and anticipate light changes.

- Know your local signal timing, especially in familiar neighbourhoods.

- Plan lane changes and turns early to avoid last-second decisions.

- Communicate expectations with anyone who borrows your car so they’re aware of responsibilities.

These habits not only help you avoid red-light camera tickets and reduce your risk of collisions!

### Give safe driving the green light

Red-light camera tickets can be surprising, but they’re not as intimidating as many drivers expect. They’re administrative fines aimed at encouraging safer driving behaviours at intersections, and they don’t impact your driving record or insurance the way police-issued tickets do. At the end of the day, safe, attentive driving is the best way to protect yourself, your passengers, and everyone on the road.

### FAQs

### How much is a red-light camera ticket in Canada?

Fines vary by province but are generally around $167- $405, depending on where you live.

### Do red-light camera tickets affect insurance rates?

No. Since they don’t go on your driving record, they usually do not impact your insurance.

### Do red-light camera tickets count as demerit points?

No. Only police-issued moving violations come with demerit points.

### Who gets the ticket if I’m driving a rental car?

The rental company receives the ticket and usually charges it back to you with an administrative fee.

### What happens if I ignore a red-light camera ticket?

You may face late fees, and may not be able to renew your licence or registration until the fine is paid.

### Do red-light cameras work at night or in bad weather?

Yes. They’re designed to function in low light, rain, snow, and other challenging conditions.

### Alberta vehicle registration: a complete guide

Before you get behind the wheel of your new car, there are a few essential steps to take, including vehicle registration. Registering a vehicle in Alberta applies whether you’ve just bought a car, just moved to the province, or have taken ownership of a family vehicle. The process is straightforward once you know what documents to bring and which steps apply to your situation. Let’s look at what you need to know before you get on the road.

### Eligibility

A person aged 18 or older can register a vehicle by themselves. A person under age 18 who is not self-supporting or married must have consent from their parent or guardian to register a vehicle for the first time. The person providing consent will sign the consent at the registry agent office.

If you are temporarily staying in Alberta for less than 183 days, and your vehicle is registered in another jurisdiction, you don’t have to register your vehicle.

### How do I register a vehicle from a dealership in Alberta?

When you purchase a vehicle from an Alberta dealership, the registration process is usually very simple. Dealerships handle most of the administrative work for you. They prepare and submit the registration documents, collect the associated fees, and assign or transfer plates.

### How do I register a used vehicle bought privately in Alberta?

To register a vehicle, you must go to a registry agent with the following:

- proof of ownership document, such as a lease, bill of sale, probated will or letters of administration

- proof of valid insurance for the vehicle that includes the name(s) of the person(s) requesting the registration

- acceptable identification

- an out-of-province, salvage or commercial inspection (if applicable)

- if you are registering a used vehicle in Alberta for the first time, you may require an inspection if the vehicle was purchased outside of Alberta

### Registering a vehicle on behalf of someone else

If you can’t go to a registry office, another person can apply for a vehicle registration, renewal, cancellation, or transfer on your behalf. The person applying on your behalf must have a valid Alberta driver’s licence or identification card. The person requesting the service on your behalf must bring:

- your proof of ownership document and proof of valid insurance

- a completed Authorization for Vehicle Services form, signed by you

- their valid Alberta driver's licence or Alberta identification card

### Registration period

Passenger, motorcycle and off-highway vehicle registrations are issued for a minimum of 1 year to a maximum of 2 years.

### How to register vehicles purchased outside of Alberta?

### Registering a vehicle purchased within Canada

If you bought the vehicle outside of Alberta, but within Canada, you’ll need to complete one of the following:

- an out-of-province inspection

- a salvage inspection

- a commercial inspection

Which inspection you complete depends on the type and status of your vehicle.

Before you go to an inspection station, you must get an Inspection Request from a registry agent.

### Out-of-province inspections

A vehicle purchased outside of Alberta, but within Canada, must pass an Alberta Out-of-Province Vehicle Inspection before it can be registered.

A new vehicle may be exempt from the out-of-province inspection requirement depending on the date of sale, mileage, and where it was purchased. Check with a registry agent to see if your new vehicle qualifies.

Off-highway vehicles such as ATVs and snowmobiles, prorate commercial vehicles, and trailers do not need an out-of-province inspection.

### Registering a vehicle purchased outside of Canada

If you bought the vehicle outside Canada, you must get the proper inspection completed and provide import documentation.

Bring your proof of ownership and the Transport Canada Vehicle Import Form – Form 1 to a registry agent and get an Inspection Request.

### Do parking tickets affect your insurance?

If you’ve ever found a parking ticket under your wiper, you’re certainly not alone. While the ticket itself is annoying, the good news is that it doesn’t affect your car insurance. While this might sound simple, there’s still a bit more to it. Understanding how parking tickets work (and what happens if you ignore them) can save you from unnecessary headaches later on.

### Why parking tickets don’t affect car insurance

Parking tickets may feel stressful in the moment, but they have no impact on your premiums because they’re not considered a Highway Traffic Act (HTA) offence. They are part of municipal bylaws, so they don’t appear on your driving record, they don’t lead to demerit points, and insurance companies simply don’t use them when calculating risk.

Your driving record is only impacted by factors that reflect how you drive. This means things like speeding, distracted driving, collisions, and other moving violations. Since parking citations don’t involve the vehicle being operated and are simply an administrative matter, they stay entirely separate when insurers are assessing premiums.

### How parking tickets can indirectly affect insurance

While a parking ticket itself won’t raise your insurance rate, ignoring one can cause a chain reaction of administrative issues that may eventually affect your ability to keep your insurance active.

Unpaid parking tickets often accumulate late fees, and in many regions, they can block you from renewing your licence or vehicle registration. If the unpaid fine is severe enough, or remains unpaid long enough, you could face a licence suspension. And once a licence is suspended, your ability to maintain insurance is affected, since insurers require a valid driver’s licence to keep your coverage active.

In some municipalities, repeated unpaid tickets may also be sent to collections. While this doesn’t directly change your insurance premiums, it could show up on your credit report, which may affect your financing options for a vehicle. In more serious cases, repeated unpaid tickets can result in towing or impoundment, leading to additional fees and the risk of driving without proper registration, which is an offence that does impact insurance.

### Parking tickets vs. Highway Traffic Act (HTA) violations: what’s the difference?

As we touched on, parking violations are treated very differently from HTA violations, and the distinction is important:

- Parking violations happen when a vehicle is stationary and generally don’t impact your driving record or insurance.

- HTA violations occur while the vehicle is in motion, and they are tracked because they reflect driving behaviour.

- Photo radar and red-light camera tickets sit somewhere in the middle. They technically involve the vehicle being in motion, but since they can’t determine the identity of the driver, they’re usually treated as administrative fines rather than driving offences. Like parking tickets, they don’t cause demerit points and generally don’t impact insurance.

### How to handle a parking ticket (paying, disputing, avoiding future tickets)

The best approach to parking tickets is to deal with them quickly so they don’t turn into bigger problems. Paying them right away avoids late fees, collections, or renewal issues. If the ticket seems incorrect, whether due to unclear signage, incorrect details, or a parking meter malfunction, you can follow your city’s dispute process. Taking photos at the scene is especially helpful when contesting!

Avoiding future tickets often comes down to staying aware of signage and time limits, setting reminders on your phone, paying close attention to street-cleaning schedules, and avoiding areas like fire hydrants, loading zones, school zones, and accessible parking spots unless you have a valid permit. Small habits like these can make a noticeable difference in preventing tickets altogether.

### Key takeaways: parking tickets and your insurance

At the end of the day, what’s important to remember is that parking tickets don’t increase your insurance or show up on your driving record, but ignoring them can lead to administrative issues that may eventually affect your ability to drive legally. Staying on top of your fines, paying them promptly, and understanding the difference between parking and moving violations can help you avoid unnecessary consequences.

### Do parking tickets go on your driving record?

No. They are not added to your driving record or driving abstract.

### What is a rent-to-own home?

Rent-to-own is a unique housing arrangement that allows you to lease a property with the option to buy it at the end of the lease period.

### How does rent-to-own work?

Rent-to-own agreements are composed of two main components: a standard lease agreement and an option to buy the home. Here's a simple breakdown of how it works:

- Lease Agreement: You start by renting the home for a set period, usually between one and three years. During this time, you make regular rent payments, just like any typical rental situation.

- Option to Purchase: Along with the lease, you get an option agreement, which gives you the right but not the obligation to buy the home once the lease ends. The price you pay to buy the home can either be set at the beginning of the lease or determined at the end, depending on the current market value.

In 2022, the number of car thefts may well be the highest we’ve seen since 2012. Most vehicles are stolen from shopping centre and supermarket parking lots—surprisingly, mainly during the daytime. Have you tried to hire a tradesperson lately to replace a part on your vehicle? If so, you’ve probably had to add significantly to your repair budget. Here’s why: inflation and a growing labour shortage.

At the start of the pandemic, some insurance companies—including belairdirect—adjusted rates to give customers some breathing room. But with things being largely back to “normal,” as well as all the factors listed above, these rates are being readjusted to reflect today’s new reality.
